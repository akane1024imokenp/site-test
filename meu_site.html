Japan is turning footsteps into electricity! The viral claim that Japan is turning footsteps into energy is true, driven by a technology called piezoelectricity. By embedding special materials into floor tiles at highly trafficked railway hubs like Tokyo and Shibuya stations, engineers can capture the mechanical pressure from millions of commuters' footsteps and convert it into electricity. However, while this technology is an innovative supplementary green energy source and a great way to promote sustainability, it currently has a relatively low electrical output and remains expensive to install and maintain. As a result, the energy harvested isn't enough to power whole cities or trains; instead, it is practically used to run localized, low-power devices in the stations like automated ticket gates and LED information boards.

On April 26, 1986, the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant near Pripyat, Ukraine, experienced the most catastrophic nuclear disaster in history. A combination of flawed reactor design and severe operational errors during a late-night safety test triggered a massive explosion and fire, releasing unprecedented amounts of radioactive material across Europe. The immediate aftermath claimed the lives of dozens of plant workers and first responders, while the long-term exposure to radiation led to severe environmental damage and widespread health consequences for the surrounding populations. Following the disaster, the government permanently evacuated over 100,000 residents to create a 1,000-square-mile Exclusion Zone that remains heavily restricted today. Though the highly radioactive reactor is now sealed beneath a massive steel containment structure, the tragedy stands as a somber milestone that profoundly altered global nuclear safety protocols.
